Their are two types of ways groundwater recharges: Natural and Artifical Recharge

Natural Recharge
Natural groundwater recharge happens through the water cycle. Mainly through precolation. The water flows into aquifers using gravity as the force to pull it through the pores and into the aquifers below us. This allows groundwater to recharge without human interactions. The benifits of using water gained from natural recharge is that it'll be flitered though many processes. If we start from the sea, evaporation will pick up the water but leave the salt behind. After that once precipitation occurs the water will either experience runoff or head straight to precolation. While gravity pulls the water to an aquifer it gets filtered through layers of rock and gravel. After that all thats left to do is to filter any bacteria left using carbon or heat. Some downsides is that the large sum of water is avaliable in short periods of time. Also, not all of the water from the precipitation ends up in an aquifer which could be biggest downside of natural groundwater recharge.
Artificial Recharge
Artificial groundwater recharge happens when water is moved using man-made systems to move water into certain aquifers to create a level of balance in where land subsidence doesn't drastically change the geography. Also it artificial recharge can also be water redirected through rivers into different places. Many benefits come out of artificial recharge. Some of them include us knowing where we want the water and controllability. Also if there have been recent results of waste affecting the soil then we can move the water to a noncontaminated area. Although the water might not get the full natural filtration process water plants can easily overcome this problem. Some downfalls are that even though there is controllability over the water it doesn’t mean the water meets California's standards for drinking water. The material of aqueducts could affect the water’s drinking capability.
